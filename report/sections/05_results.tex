\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Bandwidth metrics and cost model}
\label{sec:cost-model}

Following the convention \texttt{nccl-tests} itself uses \citep{nvidia2024ncclperf}, since this project explicitly mirrors NCCL's ring transport, two bandwidth figures are computed from every timed measurement. Algorithm bandwidth is the simple ratio $\text{algbw} = M / T$ of message size to elapsed time. Bus bandwidth rescales it by $\text{busbw} = \text{algbw} \cdot \frac{2(N-1)}{N}$, the ratio of bytes actually moved over the network to bytes of input, for any bandwidth-optimal point-to-point allreduce regardless of whether its underlying algorithm is a ring, a tree, or something else \citep{nvidia2024ncclperf}. Because that factor is baked in, busbw is comparable across different process counts against one single hardware bandwidth ceiling, which algbw alone is not.

That ceiling is estimated from this project's own data, not assumed. The Hockney latency-bandwidth model,
\begin{equation}
  T(N, M) \approx 2(N-1)\,\alpha \;+\; 2\,\frac{N-1}{N}\,M\,\beta,
  \label{eq:hockney}
\end{equation}
gives $\alpha$ (seconds of fixed per-message latency) and $\beta$ (seconds per byte, i.e. inverse bandwidth) as the two free parameters, fit independently for each algorithm variant by weighted least squares against that variant's own measured $(N, M, T)$ triples (\texttt{analysis/theoretical\_model.py}). The fit is weighted by $1/T$, turning it into a relative rather than absolute error minimization: this project's sweep spans roughly seven orders of magnitude in message size and therefore in $T$, and an unweighted fit is so dominated by the handful of largest, slowest configurations that $\alpha$, whose entire contribution to $T$ is only visible at the smallest sizes, comes out badly biased even though $\beta$ and the overall $R^2$ both look excellent. This was not a hypothetical concern: fitting the (synthetic, see Section~\ref{sec:dataset-note}) sample dataset unweighted recovered $\beta$ to within a fraction of a percent of its known generating value but missed $\alpha$ by as much as 17\%; the weighted fit recovered both to within 0.3\% on every algorithm variant. Theoretical peak bus bandwidth is reported as $1/\beta$, and efficiency at a given configuration as achieved busbw divided by that peak.

\subsection{Achieved bus bandwidth versus message size}

Figure~\ref{fig:busbw-vs-size} plots achieved busbw against message size for a representative subset of process counts, one panel per algorithm variant, with each panel's fitted $1/\beta$ shown as a dashed reference line. All three variants show the same qualitative shape the cost model predicts: a flat, near-zero region at small message sizes where busbw has not yet risen to meaningfully approach the asymptote, transitioning to a region that climbs toward, and at the largest sizes essentially reaches, the dashed theoretical peak. Table~\ref{tab:summary} quotes this concretely at the two ends of the sweep for $N=16$: busbw at 8 bytes is a small fraction of a percent of theoretical peak for every variant, while busbw at 128~MiB reaches roughly 99 to 101\% of the fitted peak (a value fractionally over 100\% simply reflects noise in that single measured cell relative to a peak fit across the whole sweep, not a violation of the model).

\subsection{Efficiency across the full sweep}

Figure~\ref{fig:efficiency-heatmap} extends this across every swept $(N, M)$ combination at once. The pattern is consistent across all three algorithm variants: efficiency is near zero across almost the entire small-message column range regardless of $N$, and rises toward 1 only once message size grows large enough that the bandwidth term of Equation~\ref{eq:hockney} dominates the latency term. The message size at which that transition happens shifts right (toward larger messages) as $N$ grows, exactly as Equation~\ref{eq:hockney} predicts: the latency term scales with $N-1$ while, at fixed busbw efficiency, the bandwidth term's threshold in $M$ does not shift down to compensate.

\subsection{Decomposing the latency floor}

Figure~\ref{fig:latency-decomposition} isolates the smallest swept message size (8 bytes) and, for the custom ring implementation specifically, breaks the cost model's prediction into its two additive terms per process count, alongside a raw \texttt{MPI\_Send}/\texttt{MPI\_Recv} ping-pong one-way latency measured on the same hardware (\texttt{apps/pingpong}, methodologically the same one-way-latency-from-round-trip convention as OSU Micro-Benchmarks' \texttt{osu\_latency} \citep{osu2024benchmarks}). At this message size the bandwidth term is negligible at every $N$ shown, essentially confirming that the modeled latency term $2(N-1)\alpha$ is the entire predicted cost once messages are small enough. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} uses the gap between that modeled term and the raw ping-pong reference to quantify, rather than merely assert, how much of the ring's small-message cost is genuine per-message latency versus rank-to-rank synchronization overhead.
