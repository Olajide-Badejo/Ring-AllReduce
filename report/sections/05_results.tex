\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Bandwidth metrics and cost model}
\label{sec:cost-model}

Following the convention \texttt{nccl-tests} itself uses \citep{nvidia2024ncclperf}, since this project explicitly mirrors NCCL's ring transport, two bandwidth figures are computed from every timed measurement. Algorithm bandwidth is the simple ratio $\text{algbw} = M / T$ of message size to elapsed time. Bus bandwidth rescales it by $\text{busbw} = \text{algbw} \cdot \frac{2(N-1)}{N}$, the ratio of bytes actually moved over the network to bytes of input, for any bandwidth-optimal point-to-point allreduce regardless of whether its underlying algorithm is a ring, a tree, or something else \citep{nvidia2024ncclperf}. Because that factor is baked in, busbw is comparable across different process counts against one single hardware bandwidth ceiling, which algbw alone is not.

That ceiling is estimated from this project's own data, not assumed. The Hockney latency-bandwidth model,
\begin{equation}
  T(N, M) \approx 2(N-1)\,\alpha \;+\; 2\,\frac{N-1}{N}\,M\,\beta,
  \label{eq:hockney}
\end{equation}
gives $\alpha$ (seconds of fixed per-message latency) and $\beta$ (seconds per byte, i.e. inverse bandwidth) as the two free parameters, fit independently for each algorithm variant by weighted least squares against that variant's own measured $(N, M, T)$ triples (\texttt{analysis/theoretical\_model.py}). The fit is weighted by $1/T$, turning it into a relative rather than absolute error minimization: this project's sweep spans roughly seven orders of magnitude in message size and therefore in $T$, and an unweighted fit is so dominated by the handful of largest, slowest configurations that $\alpha$, whose entire contribution to $T$ is only visible at the smallest sizes, comes out badly biased even though $\beta$ and the overall $R^2$ both look excellent. That failure mode was demonstrated rather than assumed: the fitting procedure was validated against a synthetic dataset with known generating constants (\texttt{results/sample\_run/generate\_\allowbreak synthetic\_sample.py}, retained solely as that validation harness), where unweighted least squares recovered $\beta$ to a fraction of a percent but missed $\alpha$ by as much as 17\%, while the $1/T$-weighted fit recovered both to within 0.3\%. That validated weighted fit is what is applied to the real measurements below. Theoretical peak bus bandwidth is reported as $1/\beta$, and efficiency at a given configuration as achieved busbw divided by that peak.

One property of the fit on this hardware needs stating up front, because it shapes how the figures should be read. On a single node, the transport underneath the point-to-point calls is shared memory, and its effective per-byte cost is not the single constant the Hockney model assumes: a reduction whose chunks fit in the 33~MB L3 cache moves bytes faster than one large enough to be bound by main-memory bandwidth. A single straight-line fit across the full 8~B to 128~MiB range therefore has genuinely limited explanatory power here, with $R^2$ of roughly 0.29 for the custom ring and 0.44 for the vendor default, far below the near-unity values the same procedure returns on the clean synthetic data. The fitted $1/\beta$ is best read not as a hard ceiling but as a sustained-bandwidth average over the whole size range; as the next figures show, the fastest cache-resident message sizes exceed it, which is a real feature of single-node shared-memory transport rather than a fitting error, and a network-fabric multi-node sweep would be expected to follow the linear model far more closely.

\subsection{Achieved bus bandwidth versus message size}

Figure~\ref{fig:busbw-vs-size} plots achieved busbw against message size for every swept process count, one panel per algorithm variant (the custom ring and the vendor's default \texttt{MPI\_Allreduce}), with each panel's fitted $1/\beta$ shown as a dashed reference line. Both variants share the same three-part shape, and it is more interesting than a simple climb to a plateau. At small message sizes busbw sits in a flat, near-zero floor where the fixed per-step latency dominates and the payload is too small to matter. It then climbs steeply and, on this single node, keeps climbing past the fitted $1/\beta$ line to a pronounced peak in the hundreds-of-kilobytes to low-megabyte range, where the working set is still L3-cache-resident: the custom ring reaches roughly 11.8~GB/s at $N=2$ near 1~MiB, and the vendor default roughly 9.8~GB/s near 512~KiB, both well above their own sustained-bandwidth asymptotes. Past that peak busbw falls again as the buffers outgrow cache and the reduction becomes bound by main-memory bandwidth, which is exactly why a single-slope fit reports the modest $R^2$ of the previous subsection. Table~\ref{tab:summary} quotes the two ends of the sweep at $N=16$: at 8~bytes both variants sit at a small fraction of a percent of the fitted peak (latency-bound), while at 128~MiB, the memory-bound far end, the custom ring reaches about 15\% and the vendor default about 23\% of their respective fitted asymptotes, both far below the cache-resident peak in between.

Comparing the two variants directly gives the report's central head-to-head result, and it is a clean crossover rather than one implementation dominating. Below roughly 4~KiB the custom ring is slower than the vendor default at every process count measured, by up to about a factor of two at the smallest sizes: this is the region where total time is set by step count, and the ring's $2(N-1)$ sequential round trips (Section~\ref{sec:step-count}) cost more latency terms than whatever lower-step algorithm the vendor selects. At and above roughly 4~KiB the ordering reverses at every process count, and the ring's bandwidth-optimal data volume makes it the faster of the two, by as much as a factor of two through the cache-resident peak (at $N=2$ near 1~MiB the ring's 11.8~GB/s against the default's 5.1~GB/s). The crossover sits at essentially the same message size across $N \in \{2,4,8,16\}$, which is consistent with it being a property of the algorithms' step-count-versus-bandwidth trade-off rather than of any one process count. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} takes up why this is the expected result and not a defect.

\subsection{Efficiency across the full sweep}

Figure~\ref{fig:efficiency-heatmap} extends this across every swept $(N, M)$ combination at once, with efficiency defined as achieved busbw over the fitted $1/\beta$ and the color scale allowed to run past 1 so the cache-resident band is not clipped away. The pattern is consistent across both variants: efficiency is near zero across the small-message columns regardless of $N$, where the latency term of Equation~\ref{eq:hockney} dominates, then rises through a bright band brighter than the $1/\beta$ reference (efficiency above 1) at the cache-resident middle sizes, then falls back below 1 at the memory-bound largest sizes. The message size at which efficiency first lifts off the floor shifts right, toward larger messages, as $N$ grows, exactly as Equation~\ref{eq:hockney} predicts: the latency term scales with $N-1$, so more of the size axis stays latency-bound at larger $N$. That a substantial region exceeds 1 is the same single-node property discussed above, the fitted asymptote is a full-range average that the cache-resident sizes beat, and is precisely what a clean network-bound sweep would not show.

\subsection{Decomposing the latency floor}

Figure~\ref{fig:latency-decomposition} isolates the smallest swept message size (8 bytes) and, for the custom ring implementation specifically, breaks the cost model's prediction into its two additive terms per process count, alongside a raw \texttt{MPI\_Send}/\texttt{MPI\_Recv} ping-pong one-way latency measured on the same hardware (\texttt{apps/pingpong}, methodologically the same one-way-latency-from-round-trip convention as OSU Micro-Benchmarks' \texttt{osu\_latency} \citep{osu2024benchmarks}). At this message size the bandwidth term is negligible at every $N$ shown, essentially confirming that the modeled latency term $2(N-1)\alpha$ is the entire predicted cost once messages are small enough. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} uses the gap between that modeled term and the raw ping-pong reference to quantify, rather than merely assert, how much of the ring's small-message cost is genuine per-message latency versus rank-to-rank synchronization overhead.
